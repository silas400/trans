Basics

2.8 million Americans identify as transgender.

The Williams Institute at UCLA estimates that about 2.8 million people in the U.S. aged 13 and older identify as transgender. This shows that transgender people make up about 1% of the US population.

Williams Institute at UCLA

Trans women and trans men differ by birth assignment.

A transgender woman is someone who was thought male at birth but lives as a woman today, while a transgender man is someone who was thought female at birth but lives as a man today. The difference lies in the sex they were assigned at birth and the gender they know themselves to be.

Mayo Clinic

Trans identities extend beyond the male–female binary.

According to the Mayo Clinic, transgender and gender-diverse terms include people whose identities don’t fit only into “male” or “female.” Some identify as neither, both, or fluid, while others express gender outside society’s expectations. These identities demonstrate that gender diversity is broader than the traditional binary framework.

Mayo Clinic

Healthcare

Transgender people face discrimination

Reports show that transgender and gender non-conforming individuals encounter disrespect, harassment, denial of treatment, and even violence when seeking medical help. These experiences make accessing care unsafe and discouraging, pushing many away from essential services.

Cancer Network National Center for Transgender Equality SAGE Journals

Many providers lack training in transgender health.

A survey of North American medical schools found the median time dedicated to LGBT-related instruction was only 5 hours across the entire curriculum, with one-third of schools reporting 0 hours during clinical training. Because of this limited education, half of transgender patients report having to teach their doctors about their care needs.

ResearchGate Center for American Progress JAMA Network

Insurance discrimination is a common barrier.

Nearly one in four transgender people report problems with insurance coverage because of their identity, and 55% who sought transition-related surgeries were denied coverage in the past year. These systemic exclusions leave many without necessary care.

U.S. Trans Survey American Medical Association

Negative provider interactions are widespread.

One-third of transgender patients report harassment or outright refusal of treatment by providers, while 15% were asked invasive, irrelevant questions about being transgender. Such experiences undermine trust and make medical visits harmful rather than healing.

NAMI Center for American Progress

Many delay or avoid care due to past harm.

Compared to cisgender people, transgender individuals are far more likely to avoid doctors because of prior discrimination or mistreatment. This avoidance contributes to worse health outcomes and deepens existing inequalities.

NCBI PMC

Mental Health

Suicide attempts are high in the transgender community.

Studies reveal that suicide attempt rates among transgender individuals range from 18–41%, with lifetime attempt rates reaching 40%, nearly nine times higher than the 4.6% U.S. average. These findings highlight a mental health crisis requiring urgent and targeted interventions.

NCBI PMC USC Global Health

Discrimination fuels poor mental health outcomes.

Experiencing stigma, harassment, or bias contributes to severe mental health disparities. Transgender adults report higher rates of depression, anxiety, and psychological distress, with 44% facing serious distress in the past month compared to only 5% of the general U.S. population.

NCBI PMC U.S. Trans Survey SpringerLink

Transgender youth face especially high suicide risk.

Young transgender individuals experience some of the most severe mental health outcomes, with 82% reporting suicidality, 40% reporting attempts, and far higher rates of non-suicidal self-injury compared to cisgender youth. These numbers underscore the vulnerability of transgender youth in unsupportive environments.

JAMA Network PubMed

Gender-affirming care protects mental health.

Protective factors such as family support, social transition, and access to gender-affirming care dramatically reduce suicide risk and improve well-being. Transgender people who socially transition report higher rates of “thriving,” and puberty blockers in adolescence are linked to lower lifetime suicidal ideation.

U.S. Trans Survey NCBI PMC AAP Publications

Violence

Family rejection often brings violence and homelessness.

Family rejection can mean physical harm and even being forced out of one’s home. For many transgender people, coming out creates immediate threats to safety and stability.

National Center for Transgender Equality

Transgender people face far higher rates of victimization.

National survey data show transgender individuals experience over four times the rate of violent victimization compared to cisgender people (86.2 vs. 21.7 per 1,000). Both trans women and men reported far higher risks than their cisgender counterparts, including rape, assault, and aggravated violence. Many attacks were perceived as hate crimes, especially among trans women.

Williams Institute at UCLA

Trans mass shooters are very rare

From 2018–2025, there were 4,147 U.S. mass shootings. Of these, only seven involved transgender suspects, or 0.17%. Since about 1% of Americans identify as transgender, the per capita rate shows they are less likely to be shooters than the general population. In contrast, 98% of mass shootings were carried out by men.

PolitiFact

Trans people are not more violent

Crime and terrorism experts told PolitiFact there is no evidence transgender people are more likely to commit gun violence or violence in general than others. The UK Parliament reported no statistically significant differences in violent convictions between trans women and other males.

PolitiFact

Inclusive bathroom access does not threaten safety

Research shows no evidence that allowing transgender individuals to use bathrooms aligning with their gender identity jeopardizes safety or privacy. Instead, transgender people often face harassment and violence when restricted and are at higher risk if forced to use bathrooms based on their sex assigned at birth.

Williams Institute at UCLA

Brain

Trans people have unique brain structures.

The largest brain imaging study of transgender people found clear differences from cisgender people in certain brain regions. The researchers concluded that transgender people do not simply shift toward male or female patterns but instead show a distinct brain profile.

PubMed

Transgender brains can resemble their experienced identity.

Although research is still in its early stages and findings vary across studies, patterns suggest that transgender people’s brains often share features with those of their identified gender. This emerging evidence highlights that aspects of brain development may align with lived gender identity, underscoring the need for further study and more informed support approaches.

Global News

Brain sex tests might not work well for trans people..

Researchers trained a brain model to predict biological sex with high accuracy in cisgender people, but when applied to transgender women, it was far less accurate. This indicates that transgender brain structures do not neatly align with traditional male or female categories.

Nature

Sports

Trans athletes do have an advantage.

Studies show the male–female performance gap, which appears at puberty and can reach 10–50% depending on the sport, is only modestly reduced after transition. Testosterone suppression in transgender women typically decreases muscle mass and strength by about 5% over 12 months, leaving much of the puberty-related advantage intact. This evidence raises concerns about whether current IOC policies ensure fair competition.

SpringerLink

Transgender athlete participation is relatively small overall.

An estimated 122,000 transgender youth play high school sports, compared to millions of total student-athletes nationwide. For context, over 7.5 million students participated in high school sports in 2018–2019, meaning transgender youth represent only a small fraction of the whole. At the college level, transgender athletes are estimated to make up less than 1.3% of athletes. This shows that while participation exists, the share is comparatively limited.

Williams Institute at UCLA

History & Culture

Pre-colonial Native tribes recognized diverse genders.

Historical accounts and scholarship show that more than 150 pre-colonial Native American tribes acknowledged third genders in their communities. These identities often had distinct cultural, social, and spiritual roles, reflecting a broader understanding of gender fluidity compared to European societies.

Human Rights Campaign

Colonialism marginalized Two-Spirit identities.

European colonizers condemned same-sex relationships and gender variance as sinful, imposing Christian norms to suppress Indigenous traditions. This resulted in marginalization for Native communities both for their ethnic identity and for expressions of gender and sexuality.

Human Rights Campaign

Gender Diversity has long been respected.

Figures like We’wha of the Zuni (Lhamana) and Osh-Tisch of the Crow (baté) lived honored third-gender roles blending men’s and women’s responsibilities. Their spiritual wisdom, craftsmanship, and bravery were celebrated, showing that identities beyond the male–female binary have deep cultural legitimacy.

Human Rights Campaign

Transgender people exist in every region of the world.

While cultural attitudes and legal recognition vary, transgender and gender-diverse people are present across societies worldwide. Legal gender recognition (LGR) highlights this reality: some countries protect self-determination, while others impose medical, legal, or even criminal barriers. From Argentina to New Zealand, South Africa to Pakistan, recent reforms show growing acknowledgment of transgender lives, even as challenges remain in many places.

Human Rights Campaign

Trans people have existed across cultures throughout history.

Although the word transgender only became common in the late 20th century, people whose gender identity differed from their sex assigned at birth have been present in every culture throughout recorded history. This shows that transgender identity is not new, but rather a deeply rooted aspect of human diversity.

Human Rights Campaign
